Shape as Substance? Materiality and Urban Africa

Issue

During the second unit of our semester, we examined African cities—past and present—through the domain of their built environment. That is, we focused on the city as a material site—a place of architectural structures, a plane of physical infrastructure, and domain of objects and things. In doing so, we entered a conversational terrain about what studying African cities as physical landscapes can tell scholars interested in African urban pasts.

 

For their second piece of public-facing writing for this course, students wrote short essays of 1000-1200 words that critically evaluated the practice of studying the city as a built environment. Their aim was to ruminate on the following questions: What does studying African cities as built environments comprised of infrastructure, buildings, and things allow historians to see? And what does it not?
