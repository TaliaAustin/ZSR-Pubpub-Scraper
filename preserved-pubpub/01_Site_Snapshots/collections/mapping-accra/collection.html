Mapping Accra

Conference


During the final unit of the Spring 2025 semester, our class concluded its consideration of African urban history by examining a single city: Accra, Ghana. Whereas our previous units have tried to look across African urban contexts in order to theorize African cities, think about the built environment, and recognize how colonial governments attempted to control urban space and urban publics, this last unit challenges us to think about how various groups of people—indigenous Ga residents, urban youth, merchants, medical personnel, British colonial officials, and Ghanaian government actors—helped give birth to a modern African city. It invites us to think about which kinds of stories, and in many ways whose stories, matter in the process of reconstructing African urban pasts.

 

For their final piece of public-facing writing, students prepared story maps that offer an interactive narrative about Accra’s recent (i.e. late 19th-century / 20th-century) history. Unlike other work prepared for this site, these story maps draw attention to specific sites / places within the city; in fact, they invite their readers along for a kind of historical ‘tour’ through various moments, processes, and places important to the city’s past. Comprised of a map, text, and visual resources, they pull us into rich and immersive stories about Accra’s history.
